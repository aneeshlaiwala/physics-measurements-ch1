Picture this: Your friend says, "My cat is SO cute!" 🐱 You ask, "Cute by HOW MUCH?" They can't really say. There's no number for cuteness, no ruler for it.

But if your friend says, "My cat is 35 cm tall and weighs 4 kg" — those are numbers we can measure. Anything you can put a number on is a Physical Quantity. ✨

📏 Length (how long), ⚖️ Mass (how heavy), ⏰ Time (how long it takes), 🌡️ Temperature (how hot/cold), 🏃 Speed (how fast) — all are physical quantities.

😎 Cuteness, anger, your love for biryani, how cool a Roblox skin looks — NOT physical quantities. (No number, no measurement!)

🧱 Now imagine LEGO bricks…

There are 2 types of LEGO pieces in the world of measurement:

  • 🟥 Basic bricks (Fundamental) — These are the OG building blocks. There are exactly 7 of them. You CAN'T break them into anything smaller. Like length, mass, time…
  • 🏰 Built things (Derived) — These are made BY combining basic bricks. Like a LEGO castle made of bricks! Area = length × length. Speed = length ÷ time. Density = mass ÷ volume.

🎯 Trick to spot them: If a quantity has a formula with × or ÷ → it's derived. If it stands alone → it's fundamental.

🎯 Physical Quantities (Detailed)

What is a Physical Quantity?

Anything that can be measured is a physical quantity. Length, mass, time, temperature, speed, area — all measurable, all physical quantities. ✅

If you can't measure it (like beauty, anger, or how cool a Roblox skin looks 😎), it's NOT a physical quantity.

💡 Real World: When mom says "the watermelon is heavier than the apple" — that's MASS being compared. When Google Maps says "destination 5 km away" — that's LENGTH. Your everyday life is full of physical quantities! IRL

🍎 Easy Explainer (Story Mode)

Imagine you have an empty water bottle. 🥤 You start pouring water in. The bottle fills up… and up… until it's FULL. The amount of water it can hold = its VOLUME.

👉 Volume = how much SPACE something takes up — or how much "stuff" you can fit inside it.

  • 🪀 A marble — tiny volume
  • 🥤 A water bottle — small volume (~500 mL)
  • 🛁 A bathtub — big volume (~250 L)
  • 🏊 A swimming pool — HUGE volume (~50,000 L)

📦 For BOX-shaped things → just multiply!

Think of it like Minecraft. 🟫 You stack blocks. The number of blocks that fit inside is the volume.

🎲 Cube (all sides equal): just multiply the side three times.
If side = 5 cm, volume = 5 × 5 × 5 = 125 cm³. Done!

📦 Cuboid (like a brick or shoebox): length × breadth × height.
Shoebox 30 cm × 20 cm × 10 cm = 6000 cm³.

🪨 For WEIRD shapes (a stone, a key) → use water!

Imagine you fill a glass almost to the brim with water. Now you drop a stone in. 💦 The water level goes UP, right?

The amount the water rose = the volume of the stone! 🤯

This is the DISPLACEMENT METHOD. The stone "displaces" (pushes away) water equal to its own volume.

📜 Story: A scientist named Archimedes figured this out 2,300 years ago while taking a bath! He saw the water rise as he sat in the tub and got SO excited he ran out yelling "EUREKA!" (means "I found it!" in Greek). 🛁⚡

😊 The water "smile" (Meniscus)

Pour water into a tall thin glass and look from the side. You'll see the water surface isn't flat — it curves. It looks like a smile 😊 (curving down in the middle).

👀 Reading rule: Always read where the BOTTOM of the smile sits. Look at it straight, with your eye at the same level as the water — not from above, not from below. (Looking from a wrong angle = wrong reading = "parallax error".)

🧊 Measurement of Volume (Detailed)

What is Volume?

Volume = the amount of space an object occupies. 📦

A cricket ball takes up more space than a marble → cricket ball has more volume. A water tank holds more water than a cup → tank has more volume.

SI Unit of Volume:
cubic metre (m³)
Smaller units → cm³, mL, L

📐 Volume of Regular Solids

Use formulas! Easy peasy.

Cube
V = side × side × side = side³
Cuboid (like a brick or box)
V = length × breadth × height

🔄 Volume Unit Conversions (BIG to small)

This is the part that trips people up. Let's nail it. 💪

Master conversion:
1 m³ = 1,000,000 cm³
(because 1 m = 100 cm, so 100³ = 1,000,000)
Liquid units:
1 L = 1000 mL = 1000 cm³
1 mL = 1 cm³
1 m³ = 1000 L
⚠️ Common confusion: Litre (L) is NOT an SI unit, but it's allowed alongside SI units. You'll see milk packets in litres and chemistry books use m³. Both are correct in their context!

🧪 Step-by-step Example: 1 cm³ = ? m³

1 cm = 1/100 m
1 cm³ = (1/100) × (1/100) × (1/100) m³
1 cm³ = 1/1,000,000 m³
1 cm³ = 0.000001 m³
✅ So 1,000,000 cm³ = 1 m³

💧 Measuring Volume of Liquids

Liquids take the shape of their container — so you measure them using a measuring cylinder (the tall glass cylinder with markings, also called a graduated cylinder).

🌊 The Meniscus Mystery

When you pour water into a glass cylinder, the surface isn't flat — it CURVES. This curve is called the meniscus.

  • 💧 Water → concave meniscus (curves DOWN in the middle, like 🥣). Read the BOTTOM of the curve.
  • 🌡️ Mercury → convex meniscus (curves UP in the middle, like 🔺). Read the TOP of the curve.
👀 Parallax Error: If you read the meniscus from above or below, you'll get the wrong number! Always keep your eye level with the meniscus. This is mega-important in chemistry labs where wrong measurements can ruin experiments. 🧪

🪨 Volume of Irregular Solids — Displacement Method

How do you measure the volume of a stone? It's not a cube or cuboid — there's no formula! 🤔

Answer: We use a trick discovered by Archimedes — the water displacement method. 🛀 EUREKA!

🧪 The Activity (from your textbook)

You need: measuring cylinder, water, a stone, a long thread.

Steps:
  1. Fill measuring cylinder with water. Note initial level: V₁ mL (say 70 mL)
  2. Tie stone to thread. Lower it gently into water (don't splash!). Don't let it overflow.
  3. Note new water level: V₂ mL (say 90 mL)

The stone's volume = V₂ - V₁ = 90 - 70 = 20 mL = 20 cm³ ✅
Volume of irregular solid:
Vstone = V₂ − V₁
🛀 Story Time: Archimedes was asked by a king to check if his crown was real gold. Sitting in his bathtub, he noticed water rising — and BOOM, he realized he could measure the crown's volume by displacement! He ran naked through the streets shouting "Eureka!" (I found it!). LEGEND

🍎 Easy Explainer (Story Mode)

Imagine you spill a bottle of paint on the floor. 🎨 The paint spreads out and covers a flat patch. The size of that patch = its AREA.

👉 Area = how much FLAT SPACE something covers on a surface.

🤔 Volume vs Area — what's the diff?

  • 📐 Area is FLAT. It's 2D. Like the top of your desk. No "thickness".
  • 📦 Volume takes up SPACE. It's 3D. Like a whole shoebox. Has thickness/height.

A piece of paper has tiny volume but BIG area. A football has big volume but smallish surface area.

📏 Real-life sizes

  • 📮 Postage stamp ≈ 4 cm²
  • 📱 Phone screen ≈ 100 cm²
  • 📓 A4 sheet ≈ 624 cm²
  • 🚪 A door ≈ 2 m²
  • 🛏️ Your bedroom floor ≈ 10–15 m²
  • 🏏 Cricket pitch ≈ 30 m²
  • 🌾 1 hectare = 10,000 m² (about 2 football fields!)

⬜ For SQUARE/RECTANGLE shapes → just multiply!

🟦 Square (all sides equal): side × side.
Tile of side 10 cm → area = 10 × 10 = 100 cm².

🟫 Rectangle (long shape): length × breadth.
Notebook 25 cm × 20 cm → area = 25 × 20 = 500 cm².

🍃 For WEIRD shapes (like a leaf) → count the squares!

Place the leaf on graph paper (the kind with little 1 cm × 1 cm squares). Trace its outline. Now COUNT the squares inside the outline:

  • ✅ Count full squares as 1 each
  • ✅ Count squares that are more than half as 1 each
  • ✅ Count squares that are exactly half as ½ each
  • ❌ Ignore squares that are less than half

Add them up. That's your area in cm²! 🎯

⬛ Measurement of Area (Detailed)

What is Area?

Area = the amount of surface covered by a closed 2D shape (a flat shape).

Floor of your room? That's an area. The screen of your phone? Area. A cricket field? You guessed it — area! 🏏

SI Unit of Area:
square metre (m²)
Smaller → cm² · Larger → km², hectare

📐 Area of Regular Shapes

Square
A = side × side = side²
Rectangle
A = length × breadth

🔄 Area Unit Conversions

1 cm² = 0.0001 m²
1 m² = 10,000 cm²
1 km² = 1,000,000 m²
1 hectare (1 hm²) = 10,000 m²

🧪 Why is 1 m² = 10,000 cm²?

1 m = 100 cm
1 m² = (100 cm) × (100 cm)
1 m² = 10,000 cm²
✅ When you square the unit, you square the conversion!
🏏 Real-world scales:
  • 📱 Phone screen → measured in cm²
  • 🏠 House floor → measured in m² or sq. ft.
  • 🌾 Farm or field → measured in hectares
  • 🌆 Whole city → measured in km²

🍃 Area of Irregular Shapes

Your hand. A leaf. A puddle. A weird coastline. None of these have formulas. 😱

Solution? The Square Centimetre Grid Method! 🟦

🧮 How the grid method works:

  1. Place the irregular shape on a graph paper (each tiny square = 1 cm²).
  2. Trace the outline of the shape onto the graph paper.
  3. Count squares using these rules:
    • ✅ Full squares inside → count as 1
    • ✅ More-than-half squares → count as 1
    • ✅ Half squares → count as ½
    • ❌ Less-than-half squares → IGNORE (count as 0)
  4. Total area ≈ (full + more-than-half + ½ × half) × 1 cm²
Approximate area of irregular shape:
A = (No. of full squares + No. of half/more-than-half squares) × 1 cm²
⚠️ "Approximate" is the keyword! The grid method gives an estimate, not an exact value. The smaller the grid squares, the more accurate the estimate. Pro tip

🍎 Easy Explainer (Story Mode)

🎒 The Two Backpack Test

Imagine two SAME-SIZED backpacks sitting on the floor:

  • 🎒 Backpack A is full of cotton balls 🌥️
  • 🎒 Backpack B is full of iron coins 🪙

Both bags are the same size (same volume). But pick them up… 🏋️

Backpack A feels light. Backpack B feels SO heavy your arm shakes! 😱

👉 Iron is DENSER than cotton. Density means: how much "stuff" is squeezed into the same amount of space.

🧮 The Magic Formula

Density = Mass ÷ Volume

Translation: "How many grams of stuff fit in 1 cubic centimetre?"

Example: A small block of iron has mass = 78 g and volume = 10 cm³.
Density = 78 ÷ 10 = 7.8 g/cm³. Means: every 1 cm³ of iron weighs 7.8 g. That's heavy! 💪

🌊 The FLOAT or SINK Rule

Water has density = 1 g/cm³. Water is the BOSS in this game. Compare your object's density to water:

  • 🦆 If your object's density is LESS than 1 → it FLOATS. (Wood = 0.65, Ice = 0.92)
  • 🪨 If it's MORE than 1 → it SINKS. (Iron = 7.8, Gold = 19.3)

That's it! Compare to 1. Done. ✅

🚢 But WAIT — How does a ship float?!

Iron has density 7.8 g/cm³. Water = 1 g/cm³. So iron should SINK, right? But ships are made of iron/steel and they FLOAT! 🤯 What gives?

Secret: A ship is mostly HOLLOW INSIDE — full of air! Air weighs almost nothing.

So when you calculate the ship's average density (steel + lots of air ÷ huge total volume), it comes out LESS than 1 g/cm³. Boom — ship floats! 🚢💨

Drop a SOLID iron block in water → sinks immediately. Make it hollow with air inside → floats. Same metal, different shape, different density.

🔺 The Density Triangle Trick

Can't remember if it's M/V or V/M? Use the magic triangle! Draw a triangle with M on top, D and V at the bottom:

  • 🙈 Cover M with your thumb → you see "D × V" → so M = D × V
  • 🙈 Cover D → you see "M / V" → so D = M ÷ V
  • 🙈 Cover V → you see "M / D" → so V = M ÷ D

Whatever you cover, the rest tells you the formula! ✨

⚖️ Measurement of Density (Detailed)

The Mind-Bender Question

"What's heavier — 1 kg of cotton or 1 kg of iron?" 🤔

Trick question! Both weigh the same — 1 kg. BUT the cotton takes up WAY more space. Why? DENSITY!

Iron packs more matter into a smaller space. Cotton spreads its matter loosely. So iron is denser than cotton.

Definition:
Density = Mass per unit Volume
D = M / V

🔺 The Density Triangle (Memory Hack!)

🧠 How to use the triangle:
  • Cover M → M = D × V
  • Cover D → D = M ÷ V
  • Cover V → V = M ÷ D
SI Unit of Density:
kg/m³ (kilogram per cubic metre)
CGS unit:
g/cm³ (gram per cubic centimetre)

🔄 Conversion: g/cm³ ↔ kg/m³

This is THE most-asked density conversion. Master it!

Imagine 1 cm³ of a substance has mass 1 g.
Now imagine a HUGE cube of 1 m³ (= 1,000,000 cm³) of same substance.
Mass = 1,000,000 × 1 g = 1,000,000 g = 1000 kg
So density = 1000 kg / 1 m³ = 1000 kg/m³
🎯 1 g/cm³ = 1000 kg/m³
Master shortcut:
×1000 → g/cm³ to kg/m³
÷1000 → kg/m³ to g/cm³
⚡ Quick check: Density of water is 1 g/cm³ = 1000 kg/m³. Memorise this — it's used in EVERY density problem!

⛵ Why Do Heavy Ships Float While a Tiny Nail Sinks?

This question stumps everyone the first time. Let's crack it. 💡

The answer: AVERAGE density

An iron nail is solid iron all the way through. Its density (~7.8 g/cm³) is HIGHER than water (1 g/cm³). It sinks. ⬇️

A steel ship is mostly hollow! It contains tons of air inside. Its average density (steel + air) is LESS than water. So it floats. ⬆️🚢

📜 Floating Rule:
  • If density of object < density of liquid → FLOATS 🐟
  • If density of object > density of liquid → SINKS 🪨
  • If equal → object stays suspended 😐
🛩️ Real-world impact: Knowing density is CRUCIAL for designing ships, airplanes, submarines and helium balloons. Get the density wrong and your ship sinks like the Titanic! ⚠️

🧪 How to Find Density (Experimentally)

For a Solid (regular shape, e.g. cube):

  1. Measure mass using a balance.
  2. Use a ruler to measure dimensions.
  3. Calculate volume using formulas (side³, l×b×h, etc.)
  4. Density = Mass ÷ Volume

For an Irregular Solid (e.g. stone):

  1. Measure mass on a balance (= M).
  2. Pour water into measuring cylinder, note V₁.
  3. Drop the stone in, note V₂.
  4. Volume of stone = V₂ - V₁
  5. Density = M ÷ (V₂ - V₁)
Density of irregular solid:
D = M / (V₂ − V₁)

Imagine you and your friend race from one end of the playground to the other. 🏃‍♂️🏃‍♀️ You finish in 10 seconds. Your friend finishes in 15 seconds. You won!

Why? Because you were FASTER. You covered the same distance in LESS time. That's SPEED.

Speed = Distance ÷ Time

"How much ground you cover per second/minute/hour"

🐢🚀 Real-life speeds (from slow to insanely fast)

  • 🐌 Snail: 0.05 km/h (super slow)
  • 🚶 Walking: 5 km/h
  • 🚴 Cycling: 20 km/h
  • 🏃 Usain Bolt (world's fastest human): 44 km/h
  • 🐆 Cheetah: 100 km/h
  • 🚗 City car: 50–80 km/h
  • 🚄 Bullet train: 320 km/h
  • 🏎️ F1 race car: 350 km/h
  • ✈️ Jet plane: 900 km/h
  • 🔊 Sound: 1,235 km/h
  • 💡 Light: 1,080,000,000 km/h (the universe's speed limit! 🤯)

🪄 The 18/5 Magic Trick

India uses km/h on roads (cars, buses). But scientists prefer m/s. So you'll often need to convert. Here's the magic:

🔄 km/h → m/s : multiply by 5/18 (you go SLOWER number)
🔄 m/s → km/h : multiply by 18/5 (you go BIGGER number)

👉 Trick: Going from km/h to m/s, the number gets smaller. So you multiply by the smaller fraction (5/18).

Why 18 and 5? Because 1 km = 1000 m and 1 hour = 3600 s. So 1 km/h = 1000/3600 = 5/18 m/s. Just simplification! 🎯

Quick examples:

  • 72 km/h → 72 × 5/18 = 20 m/s
  • 108 km/h → 108 × 5/18 = 30 m/s
  • 10 m/s → 10 × 18/5 = 36 km/h

🎢 Two Types of Motion

🟢 Uniform speed: Same speed all the time. Like a clock's second-hand — always 6° per second. Boring but predictable!

🟡 Non-uniform speed: Speed keeps changing. Like a car in city traffic — speeding up at green lights, slowing down at speed-breakers, stopping at red lights. Real life is mostly non-uniform!

For non-uniform motion, we use AVERAGE speed = total distance ÷ total time. (Doesn't matter how fast or slow you went at each moment.)

🚀 Speed (Detailed)

What is Speed?

Speed = how fast (or slow) an object is moving — regardless of direction.

Or formally: The distance travelled by an object in unit time.

Or even more formally: The rate of change of distance.

The Speed Formula 🔥
Speed = Distance ÷ Time
v = d / t
SI Unit of Speed:
metre per second (m/s)
Other common units:
km/h, km/min, cm/s

🐢 vs 🐆 Uniform vs Non-Uniform Speed

  • Uniform speed: Speed stays SAME throughout the journey. (e.g., a car cruising at exactly 60 km/h on a highway)
  • Non-uniform / variable speed: Speed CHANGES during journey. (e.g., a car in city traffic — slow, fast, stop, go!)
🏃‍♂️ World record! Usain Bolt finished the 100m race in 9.58 seconds at the 2009 Olympics. That's roughly 37.6 km/h average — faster than most city buses! 🚌💨

🔄 Conversion: km/h ↔ m/s

This is THE most-asked speed conversion. Get it right and you're golden. ✨

🧮 Deriving 1 km/h = 5/18 m/s

1 km = 1000 m
1 hour = 3600 s
1 km/h = 1000 m ÷ 3600 s
1 km/h = 1000/3600 m/s
1 km/h = 5/18 m/s ≈ 0.278 m/s
🎯 To convert km/h → m/s, multiply by 5/18

🧮 Deriving 1 m/s = 18/5 km/h

Just flip the previous result!
1 m/s = 18/5 km/h = 3.6 km/h
🎯 To convert m/s → km/h, multiply by 18/5 (or just by 3.6)
⚡ Cheat code:
km/h → m/s: × 5/18
m/s → km/h: × 18/5 (or × 3.6)

⚖️ Comparing Speeds

How do you compare two objects moving at different speeds? Two ways:

Method 1 — Same time, more distance wins:
If both run for 1 hour, whoever covers more distance is faster. Easy
Method 2 — Same distance, less time wins:
If both run 100 m, whoever takes less time is faster. Race vibe

🐎🐪 Example from your textbook

A horse covers 120 km in 2 hours. A camel covers 80 km in 6 hours. Who's faster?

Speed of horse = 120 ÷ 2 = 60 km/h
Speed of camel = 80 ÷ 6 ≈ 13.33 km/h
🐎 Horse wins! 60 km/h is way faster than 13.33 km/h

📖 Key Definitions — All in One Place!

Memorise these like Pokémon stats. They show up in EVERY exam. 📜

🎯 Quantities

Physical Quantity
Anything that can be measured. Examples: length, mass, time, speed, area, volume, density.
Fundamental (Base) Quantities
Quantities that cannot be expressed in terms of any other physical quantities. There are 7 in the SI system.
Derived Quantities
Physical quantities that are defined in terms of fundamental quantities (e.g., area = length × breadth).
SI System
The International System of Units — the global standard set of units used in science and measurement.
CGS System
A system of units based on centimetre (length), gram (mass), and second (time).

🧊 Volume

Volume
The amount of space occupied by an object. SI unit: cubic metre (m³).
Capacity
The maximum volume of a liquid that can be stored in a container. The capacity of the container can be changed by increasing/decreasing the quantity of matter inside.
Measuring Cylinder
A cylindrical glass or plastic jar with markings (graduations), used to measure volume of liquids accurately. Comes in capacities from 10 mL to 1000 mL.
Meniscus
The curve at the surface of a liquid in a measuring cylinder. Water → concave (read bottom). Mercury → convex (read top).
Parallax Error
An error in reading caused by viewing the meniscus from an angle. Avoided by keeping eye level with the meniscus.
Displacement Method
A technique to measure volume of irregular solids by noting the rise in water level when the solid is immersed.

⬛ Area

Area
The amount of surface covered by a closed (2D) shape. SI unit: square metre (m²).
Regular Shape
A shape (square, rectangle, triangle, circle) whose area can be calculated using a definite formula.
Irregular Shape
A shape (leaf, hand, coastline) without fixed dimensions. Area is approximated using a 1 cm² grid.
Hectare
A unit for measuring large areas. 1 hectare = 1 hectometre × 1 hectometre = 100 m × 100 m = 10,000 m².

⚖️ Density

Density
The mass per unit volume of a substance. Density (D) = Mass (M) / Volume (V).
SI Unit of Density
kilogram per cubic metre (kg/m³). CGS unit: gram per cubic centimetre (g/cm³).
Conversion (g/cm³ → kg/m³)
1 g/cm³ = 1000 kg/m³. Multiply by 1000 to convert from g/cm³ to kg/m³.
Floats / Sinks
An object floats if its density < density of liquid. It sinks if its density > density of liquid.

🚀 Speed

Speed
The distance travelled by an object in unit time. Speed = Distance / Time. SI unit: m/s.
Uniform Speed
When an object moves at the same speed throughout its journey.
Non-uniform / Variable Speed
When the speed of an object changes during its journey.
Conversion (km/h → m/s)
Multiply by 5/18. Conversely, m/s → km/h: multiply by 18/5 (or 3.6).

⛵ Float / Sink Rule

  • D_object < D_liquid → FLOATS
  • D_object > D_liquid → SINKS
  • D_object = D_liquid → suspended
  • Wood floats (lighter than water)
  • Iron sinks (denser than water)
  • Ship floats (avg density < water)

🧩 Riddle 1
"I'm flat as a pancake, height I have none. Multiply length by breadth — and you'll have me done. What am I?"
🎯 AREA! Flat 2D, calculated as length × breadth.
🧩 Riddle 2
"Ice and water are made of the SAME stuff (H₂O). Yet ice FLOATS in water. How is that even possible?"
When water freezes, its molecules arrange in a CRYSTAL pattern with extra space between them.
So ice has MORE volume but SAME mass → density goes DOWN.
Ice density = 0.92 g/cm³, water = 1.00 g/cm³.
🎯 Ice is LESS DENSE than water → it floats! That's also why icebergs only show 10% of themselves above water. 🧊🌊
🧩 Riddle 3 (Classic Brain Buster!)
"A boat is floating in a swimming pool. The boat carries a heavy stone. The sailor throws the stone overboard into the pool. Does the water level in the pool go UP, DOWN, or stay the SAME?"
When the stone is on the BOAT, it makes the boat sit lower. The boat (with stone) displaces water equal to its TOTAL WEIGHT (stone is heavy → lots of water displaced).
When you throw the stone INTO the water, the stone now displaces water equal to only its OWN VOLUME (which is small — stone is dense, so it has small volume for its weight).
Water displaced when stone was on boat = MORE.
Water displaced when stone is at bottom = LESS.
🎯 Water level goes DOWN! 🤯 Mind blown? This is one of the most famous physics puzzles!
🧩 Riddle 4
"A glass is filled to the BRIM with water and an ice cube floats in it. As the ice cube melts, will the water OVERFLOW, stay the SAME, or LOWER?"
A floating ice cube displaces water equal to its OWN MASS.
When it melts, the same mass of ice becomes the same mass of water.
That water EXACTLY fills the space the submerged part of ice was occupying!
🎯 Water stays at the SAME level! No overflow! 🌊 (This is why melting Arctic ice doesn't directly raise sea levels — only land ice melting does.)
🧩 Riddle 5
"I'm exactly 7 in number. I'm the OG of measurements. Length and mass and time are 3 of me — name 2 more!"
The 7 fundamental quantities (in SI):
1. Length (m), 2. Mass (kg), 3. Time (s), 4. Temperature (K)
5. Electric current (A), 6. Luminous intensity (cd), 7. Amount of substance (mol)
🎯 Any 2 of: Temperature, Electric current, Luminous intensity, Amount of substance!
🧩 Riddle 6 (Tricky!)
"Two friends run a race. Aman runs 100 m in 12 s. Boman runs 200 m in 25 s. Who is FASTER?"
You can't compare directly — different distances and times. Calculate SPEED for each!
Aman: 100 ÷ 12 = 8.33 m/s
Boman: 200 ÷ 25 = 8.00 m/s
🎯 AMAN is faster! Even though Boman ran longer distance, Aman covered ground at a higher rate. 🏃‍♂️💨
🧩 Riddle 7
"I can hold 1000 of my smaller cousin. My SI symbol is just one capital letter. I'm what your milk and juice are measured in. Who am I?"
🎯 L (litre)! 1 L = 1000 mL. (Bonus: 1 L = 1000 cm³ too!) 🥛
🧩 Riddle 8 (Super Tricky!)
"A 1 kg block of GOLD and a 1 kg block of FEATHERS are dropped into water at the same time. Which one displaces MORE water?"
Both have SAME mass (1 kg). But mass ≠ volume!
Gold density = 19.3 g/cm³ → 1 kg of gold = ~52 cm³ (tiny!)
Feathers density ≈ 0.0025 g/cm³ → 1 kg of feathers = ~400,000 cm³ (HUGE!)
When fully submerged, each displaces water equal to its OWN VOLUME.
🎯 FEATHERS displace WAY more water! They're far less dense, so 1 kg takes up much more space. 🪶 (Of course, feathers float so they don't actually sink completely — but if you forced them under!)
